## John Caples -- Deep Research

### Origin Story

John Caples (1900-1990) was a U.S. Naval Academy graduate who pivoted from engineering to copywriting in 1925. Within his first year at Ruthrauff & Ryan, he wrote what became the most famous advertisement headline in history: "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano But When I Started to Play!" for the U.S. School of Music. The ad was a mail-order piece, meaning every single response was trackable -- Caples could count exactly how many people clipped the coupon and mailed it in. This empirical feedback loop became the foundation of his entire career. He joined BBDO in 1927 and stayed for 49 years, eventually becoming vice president. His method was radical for the era: run the same advertisement with different headlines and different return addresses in the same publication, then count which address received more responses. This wasn't opinion-based creative direction -- it was measurement science applied to copywriting. He published his findings in "Tested Advertising Methods" (1932), which went through five editions over 66 years. David Ogilvy called him "one of the most effective copywriters who has ever lived" and "an indomitable analyzer and teacher of advertising." He was inducted into both the Copywriters Hall of Fame (1973) and the American Advertising Federation Hall of Fame (1977-1978).

### Core Methodology (5-7 numbered actionable principles)

1. **The headline IS the ad.** The headline accounts for 50-75% of an advertisement's total effectiveness. A good headline makes writing the rest relatively easy; a poor headline means nobody reads the copy no matter how brilliant it is. Caples: "Come up with a good headline, and you're almost sure to have a good ad. But even the greatest writer can't save an ad with a poor headline."

2. **Self-interest is the strongest force in advertising.** Of all headline qualities tested across decades, those that appeal to the reader's self-interest (what's in it for them, the benefit they'll receive) outperformed every other type. This is not a suggestion -- it was proven across thousands of split tests. Every headline should answer the reader's implicit question: "What do I get?"

3. **Test everything; trust nothing.** Never rely on opinion, gut feeling, or committee consensus to judge whether an ad will work. Run the ad. Measure the response. Caples: "Every single element in an advertisement -- headline, subhead, photo, and copy -- must be put there not because it looks good, not because it sounds good, but because testing has shown that it works best."

4. **Be specific, not vague.** Saying "52.7%" is more credible and more effective than saying "more than half." Specificity signals authenticity and builds trust. One advertiser saw demand drop dramatically when they replaced specific statistics with vague claims.

5. **Write many headlines, then test the best.** Caples advocated writing at least 10 headline variations for any advertisement, then testing the top candidates against each other. The difference between the best and worst headline for the same product could be 19.5x in response rate.

6. **Long headlines that say something outpull short headlines that say nothing.** Never sacrifice clarity for brevity. A headline's job is to stop the reader with a believable promise -- if it takes 15 words to make that promise clear, use 15 words.

7. **Overwrite, then trim.** If you need a thousand words, write two thousand. Cut vigorously. Fact-packed messages carry a wallop. This applies to headlines too -- draft expansively, then compress to the essential promise.

### Decision Rules (MOST IMPORTANT -- if/then rules for which headline type to use when)

- If you have a clear, strong benefit the reader will receive, then use a **Self-Interest headline** (benefit-focused or keyword formula). This is the default -- use it unless you have a specific reason not to. Examples: "How To [achieve desired outcome]," "Get rid of [pain point] for good," testimonial headlines.

- If you are launching something new (product, feature, version, improvement), then use a **News headline** beginning with "Introducing," "Announcing," "New," "Now," "At Last," or a date. News headlines are the second-strongest type after self-interest.

- If you can combine news AND self-interest, then do so -- the combination is more powerful than either alone. Example: "Announcing a New Way to [benefit]" or "New [product] Helps You [desired outcome]."

- If you want to use curiosity, then ALWAYS pair it with self-interest or news. Curiosity alone consistently failed in Caples's testing. Headlines that provoke curiosity without delivering substance were among the worst performers.

- If you are selling something with a genuinely low price or discount, then use a **Price headline** -- but sparingly. Overusing price headlines conditions your audience to wait for deals and creates a discount death-spiral. Reserve for genuine promotions, not as a default.

- If you are writing for a specific, identifiable audience segment, then **address them directly in the headline.** "To the man who will settle for nothing less than the presidency" performs better than a generic benefit headline because it self-selects the right reader.

- If your product has a "quick and easy" angle (fast results, simple method), then use the **Quick/Easy formula** -- "How I Improved My Memory in One Evening." People love shortcuts. But the claim must be believable.

- If you have a compelling personal story or transformation, then use the **Story/Testimonial headline.** "How a Fool Stunt Made Me a Star Salesman" combines curiosity and self-interest.

- If you are stuck and cannot generate ideas, then default to a **Keyword headline** starting with "How To," "How," "Why," "Which," "Who Else," "Wanted," "This," "Because," "If," or "Advice." These are the workhorse formulas -- they never wear out because the reader's curiosity about their own interests is inexhaustible.

- If you are tempted to write a one-to-three word headline, then DON'T -- unless you are an expert. Short headlines must accomplish everything longer ones do naturally (grab attention, promise a benefit, arouse curiosity) in far fewer words. Most short headlines fail because they are vague or clever without being clear.

- If the headline is negative in tone (warns of loss, danger, embarrassment), then make sure it points toward a solution. Pure negativity without a benefit was a common trait of failed headlines. But "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?" works because the implied benefit is: read this and stop embarrassing yourself.

- If you catch yourself writing something clever, abstract, or punny, then STOP. Headlines that are clever but don't promise a benefit or deliver news consistently failed. "The promise must be believable. Never make your claim bigger than your proof."

### Key Concepts and Vocabulary (table)

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Self-interest | The strongest headline quality. Appeals directly to what the reader wants, needs, or fears. Features tell, benefits sell. |
| News | The second-strongest quality. Announces something new -- product, use, discovery. Triggers the human desire to know what's new. |
| Curiosity | The third quality. Powerful hook but ONLY when combined with self-interest or news. Alone, it consistently fails. |
| Quick/Easy Way | The fourth quality. Suggests a fast, simple path to something the reader wants. Must be believable. |
| Split-run test | Running two versions of the same ad (differing only in headline) in the same publication with different return addresses, then counting responses. Caples's primary testing method. |
| Pulling power | The measurable response rate of an ad -- how many people it "pulls" to take action (clip a coupon, write a letter, call a number). |
| Blind response | A test where the reader doesn't know they're being tested. Different return addresses on otherwise identical ads. |
| Keyed ad | An ad with a unique identifier (code, address, phone number) so responses can be tracked back to the specific headline/layout that generated them. |
| Workhorse headlines | The 10 keyword-formula headlines (How To, Why, Which, etc.) that reliably perform and can be used repeatedly without wearing out. |
| Discount death-spiral | The trap of over-relying on price headlines, which trains customers to wait for sales and erodes brand value. |
| First-person story appeal | Caples's signature style -- headlines written as personal narratives ("How I..." / "They Laughed When I...") that combine curiosity and self-interest. |

### Specific Techniques (the actual headline formulas enumerated with examples)

**Category 1: Keyword Headlines (10 formulas) -- "Workhorse" headlines. Use when stuck. Never wear out.**

1. **Begin with "How To"** -- "How to Win Friends and Influence People"
2. **Begin with "How"** -- "How I Improved My Memory in One Evening"
3. **Begin with "Why"** -- "Why Some People Almost Always Make Money in the Stock Market"
4. **Begin with "Which"** -- "Which of These $2.50 to $5 Best Sellers Do You Want -- for Only $1 Each?"
5. **Begin with "Who Else"** -- "Who Else Wants a Whiter Wash -- with No Hard Work?"
6. **Begin with "Wanted"** -- "Wanted -- Your Services as a High-Paid Real Estate Specialist"
7. **Begin with "This"** -- "This Is Marie Antoinette -- Riding to Her Death"
8. **Begin with "Because"** -- "Because of a Remarkable Experience -- This Book Is Being Offered Free"
9. **Begin with "If"** -- "If You Are a Careful Driver, You Can Save Money on Car Insurance"
10. **Begin with "Advice"** -- "Advice to Wives Whose Husbands Don't Save Money"

**Category 2: Benefit-Focused Headlines (9 formulas) -- Highest conversion. Master these first.**

11. **Use a Testimonial Headline** -- "I Was Broke and Desperate Until I Discovered This Amazing System"
12. **Offer the Reader a Test** -- "Can Your Kitchen Pass the Guest Test?"
13. **Offer Information of Value** -- "Free Book Tells You 12 Secrets of Better Lawn Care"
14. **Tell a Story** -- "How a Fool Stunt Made Me a Star Salesman"
15. **Warn the Reader to Delay Buying** -- "Don't Buy a Car Until You've Read This"
16. **Let the Advertiser Speak Directly to the Reader** -- first-person voice throughout the ad
17. **Address Your Headline to a Specific Person or Group** -- "To the Man Who Will Settle for Nothing Less Than the Presidency"
18. **Have Your Headline Ask a Question** -- "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?"
19. **Offer Benefits Through Facts and Figures** -- "The Deaf Now Hear Whispers"

**Category 3: News Headlines (8 formulas) -- Strong when you have genuine news. Second-best type.**

20. **Begin with "Introducing"** -- "Introducing the New Gillette Sensor"
21. **Begin with "Announcing"** -- "Announcing a New Dictionary of Gardening Terms"
22. **Use Words with Announcement Quality** -- "Finally," "Presenting," "Just Released"
23. **Begin with "New"** -- "New Floss Has Toothpaste Built In So You Don't Need to Brush"
24. **Begin with "Now"** -- "Now You Can Travel to Paris for $99"
25. **Begin with "At Last"** -- "At Last -- a Way to End Thinning Hair"
26. **Begin with "Finally"** -- "Finally, a Computer That's Simple Enough for Anyone"
27. **Put a Date into Your Headline** -- "Coming September 7th, the Hottest TV Show the Critics Are Raving About"
28. **Write Your Headline in News Style** -- write as if it were a newspaper article lead

**Category 4: Price Headlines (5 formulas) -- Use sparingly. Risk of discount death-spiral.**

29. **Feature the Price** -- "All This for Only $29.95"
30. **Feature Reduced Price** -- "Was $100, Now $49"
31. **Feature a Special Merchandising Offer** -- "Buy One, Get One Free"
32. **Feature an Easy Payment Plan** -- "Only $5 a Month"
33. **Feature a Free Offer** -- "Free -- 30-Day Trial Membership"

**Category 5: Short Headlines (3 formulas) -- Experts only. High failure rate.**

34. **One-Word Headline** -- Rarely works. Requires the single word to carry benefit, news, and curiosity simultaneously.
35. **Two- or Three-Word Headline** -- Same constraint. Most marketers who attempt these produce vague, clever-sounding phrases that fail to stop readers.

### Caples's Analysis of WHY Headlines Fail (from Chapter 4)

Caples examined 10 unsuccessful headlines and identified these common failure traits:
- All primarily aroused curiosity WITHOUT delivering substance or benefit
- None provided news or significant benefits to the reader
- A majority had negative connotations without pointing toward a solution
- They were often "clever" or abstract rather than clear and specific
- They assumed the reader would care enough to figure out what the ad was about

Remember that every headline has one job. It must stop your prospects with a believable promise."** -- 1978 NY Times tribute ad (full-page ad celebrating Caples's induction into the Advertising Hall of Fame), via infomarketingblog.com/Caples.pdf

2. **"Times change. People don't. Words like 'free' and 'new' are as potent as ever. Ads that appeal to a reader's self-interest still work. The subjects that fascinated people fifty years ago still fascinate them today."** -- 1978 NY Times tribute ad, same source

3. **"I have seen one mail-order advertisement actually sell not twice as much, not three times as much, but nineteen and a half times as much merchandise as another ad for the same product. Both were run in the same publication. Both had photographic illustrations. Both had carefully written copy. The difference was that one used the right headline and the other used the wrong headline."** -- Tested Advertising Methods, cited in SOFII review by Ken Burnett (sofii.org)
