## Gary Halbert -- Deep Research

### Origin Story

Gary C. Halbert (1938-2007) was a broke, obsessive mail-order entrepreneur in Ohio in the mid-1960s who spent three years following every "proven" direct mail rule -- bulk rate postage, teaser copy on envelopes, four-color brochures, printed order cards -- and failing repeatedly. His wife and he would stuff letters while sitting in a house with no water or electricity because he had spent the utility money on test mailings.

The breakthrough came when he forced himself to think: "What if my life literally depended on the next mailing working?" That constraint-driven thought experiment made him reject every "proven" rule. He reasoned that if he would be killed for failure, he would never mail bulk rate (risk of non-delivery), never use a label (looks impersonal), never put teaser copy on the envelope (screams commercial), and never include brochures or printed inserts visible upon opening (triggers the "Oh, yuck!" reaction). Instead, he would send a plain white envelope with a live stamp, a typed address, no company name, and inside -- just a letter that looked personally typed.

He then read a newspaper story about a woman who researched family coats of arms and mailed postcards to everyone with that surname. This gave him the idea for "fake computer letters" -- typing one letter per surname (e.g., "Dear Mr. Anderson") with the name woven throughout the copy, then offset-printing thousands. Combined with his A-pile envelope format, this became the famous Coat of Arms letter: 361 words, one page, which generated 7.3 million cash-with-order customers, employed 700+ people, and required 40 staff just to process 20,000 checks per day in bank deposits.

He later served time at Boron Federal Prison Camp (1984) for mail-related business violations tied to scaling too fast (unfulfilled orders). From prison, he wrote 25 letters to his son Bond -- The Boron Letters -- which codified his entire methodology and remain freely available at thegaryhalbertletter.com.

### Core Methodology (5-7 numbered actionable principles)

1. **Get delivered, get opened, get read -- in that order.** The hierarchy of direct mail success is not about what the copy says but whether it reaches the recipient, gets opened, and gets read. Most mail fails at step one or two, never reaching the copy at all. "What does it matter how sparkling your copy, how compelling your offer, or how attractive your price if your intended recipient never receives (or opens) your letter?"

2. **A-pile or die.** Every person sorts mail into two piles over a trash can. The A-pile (personal-looking mail) always gets opened. The B-pile (obviously commercial) mostly gets trashed. The single most important design decision is making your piece look like A-pile mail: live stamps, typed/handwritten addressing, no company name on return address, no teaser copy, no visible brochures or commercial materials when opened.

3. **Find the starving crowd before you create the product.** The #1 mistake is developing a product first, then looking for a market. Instead, find a group of people with a proven, desperate desire -- observable through what they already spend money on. "If you and I both opened hamburger stands and you could have any advantage, what would it be? The only advantage I want is a starving crowd."

4. **The offer is the heart and soul of copywriting.** "Strong copy will not overcome a weak offer." The offer (the deal, the proposition) matters more than the writing. Spend more time sweetening the offer than on any other aspect of copy creation. Stack value until saying "no" feels irrational: core product + bonuses + guarantee + urgency + risk reversal.

5. **Follow the AIDA sequence -- Attention, Interest, Desire, Action.** Structure every piece sequentially: grab attention (grabbers, headlines, pattern interrupts), build interest (feed interesting facts), create desire (describe benefits, paint mental pictures of ownership), compel action (be extremely specific: tell them exactly what to do, where the order form is, how much to send, who to make the check out to, and to do it RIGHT NOW).

6. **Prep is 90% of the work; writing is the last 10%.** Create three tools before writing a single word of copy: (a) a detailed Fact Sheet about the product, (b) a Benefits List translating every fact into what it does for the buyer, (c) a Deal Sheet reducing the strongest offer to a few lines on the back of an envelope. Then immerse in your swipe file of proven winners. The "central selling idea" will emerge from this preparation.

7. **Test cheaply, then scale winners.** "Most new marketing ideas fail. That's OK. You just keep swinging the bat till you hit a home run and then, once you've got your winner, you simply milk it for all it's worth." Test with the smallest viable quantity. Mail first class even when planning a bulk rollout. Find out cheaply whether the idea has market viability before investing in scale.

### Decision Rules (MOST IMPORTANT -- if/then rules)

**Envelope / Outer Appearance Decisions:**

| IF | THEN |
|---|---|
| Your life depended on this mailing working | You would NEVER mail bulk rate, NEVER use a label, NEVER put teaser copy on the envelope, NEVER show a company name on the corner card |
| You are choosing between bulk rate and first class for a test | ALWAYS test first class. Even if you plan to roll out bulk, test at first class to get a true read on the offer's power |
| Your unit of sale is $39.95+ and the package weighs 1 oz or less | Stop mailing bulk. Switch to first class permanently |
| You are designing the envelope | Make it pass the "would a busy person sort this into their personal mail pile?" test. If any element screams commercial, remove it |
| The recipient opens the envelope | They must NOT see brochures, order cards, or printed inserts first. They must see what looks like a personal letter. Hide commercial materials in a sealed inner envelope or behind the letter. Timing is everything -- "selling is like seduction" |

**List / Market Selection Decisions:**

| IF | THEN |
|---|---|
| You have not identified a "starving crowd" | Do NOT write copy yet. Find the market first. The best copy in the world cannot sell to people who don't want what you're offering |
| You are evaluating mailing lists | Rank by: (1) Recency -- most recent buyers first, always, (2) Frequency -- repeat buyers over one-time, (3) Unit of sale -- higher spenders indicate stronger desire. Recency is by far the most important |
| You are choosing between a compiled list and a buyer list | ALWAYS choose proven mail-order buyers over compiled names. A list of people who have bought similar products by mail will outperform any compiled list |
| You see the same ad or offer running repeatedly | That advertiser has hit a nerve. Consider entering that same market. "You don't have to guess. You don't have to wonder. All you have to do is observe." |
| You have a "great idea" for a product | Test market viability FIRST with the smallest possible mailing. Budget ~$0.50 per piece. If 2,600 letters produce near-zero response, the market is telling you something -- move on cheaply |

**Copy Structure Decisions:**

| IF | THEN |
|---|---|
| You are about to start writing copy | STOP. Have you completed the Fact Sheet, Benefit List, and Deal Sheet? If not, do those first. "If you do your research properly, your ads and sales letters will almost write themselves" |
| You need to grab attention | Attach a physical object ("grabber") that ties directly into the copy. Coins, dollar bills, bags of dirt, sand -- anything that creates curiosity and connects to the letter's theme. The grabber MUST be relevant to the message or the reader will feel insulted |
| You are writing the body copy | Follow AIDA strictly. Do NOT skip steps. Do NOT ask for the sale before building interest and desire. "If you ask a woman to go to bed with you immediately upon being introduced, she will think you are a creep" |
| You are closing / asking for the order | Devote up to 25% of the entire piece to closing. Be absurdly specific: tell them where the order form is, tell them to fill it out, tell them to enclose payment, tell them how much, tell them who to make the check out to, tell them to mail it, tell them to do it TODAY |
| You are writing bullets (fascinations) | "Twist" the facts -- tease the reader with a glimpse of the benefit without revealing the full answer. The reader must feel they can only satisfy their curiosity by buying. "Many times a person will order just because one of them appealed to him" |
| You are deciding on guarantee length | Use the longest guarantee possible (1 year minimum). Counter-intuitively, the longer the refund window, the FEWER refunds you get, because the urgency to act on the guarantee fades |
| Your copy is finished | Apply the "so what" test to every claim. Set it aside for a day, then rework using AIDA sequence: (1) attention-getting opening, (2) expand on central selling idea with interesting facts, (3) paint mental pictures of benefit-of-ownership, (4) extremely specific action steps with urgency |

**Offer / Pricing Decisions:**

| IF | THEN |
|---|---|
| You are setting the test price | Offer the lowest price that could conceivably make a profit. For a first test, maximize response data over margin |
| You are choosing payment terms for a first test | Use "Send No Money -- We'll Bill You Later" to maximize response. Test more restrictive terms only after proving the offer works |
| You want to increase response without changing copy | Review Halbert's 20-point checklist: live stamps, direct-impression addressing, remove company name from corner card, remove teaser copy, add "RUSH" in red, wider margins + subheads + bold for eye relief, hide or remove brochure, extend guarantee to 1 year, add photo of letter writer, print order coupon on yellow paper, include phone number in body copy, mail first class, double-team everything (use multiple vendors, keep everyone competitive) |

### Specific Techniques (step-by-step, concrete)

**Technique 1: Creating an A-Pile Direct Mail Piece (step-by-step)**

1. Select a plain white #10 envelope. No color, no windows, no odd sizes.
2. Address it by direct impression (heat transfer, laser) or typewriter. Never use a label.
3. Affix a live first-class postage stamp. Never use a meter, printed indicia, or bulk-rate stamp.
4. Corner card (return address): sender's name and address ONLY. No company name, no logo, no tagline.
5. No teaser copy anywhere on the envelope. Nothing printed on it except the address and return address.
6. Print "RUSH" in red ink on the envelope (optional but tested to increase response).
7. Inside: a letter that looks personally typed. Dated with day, time, date. Personal salutation ("Dear Mr. Jones").
8. All commercial materials (order form, brochure, reply envelope) go inside a sealed plain white inner envelope with a typed note: "Please open this envelope as soon as you have read my letter."
9. The recipient sees ONLY the letter when they open the outer envelope. The commercial materials are discovered only after they have been "warmed up" by the letter.

**Technique 2: The Copy Creation Process (Halbert's full sequence)**

1. Assemble a research file: list cards, product samples, competitor mailings, any relevant material.
2. Read everything and take "nugget notes" -- one fact/insight per line.
3. Star the best nuggets: * for good, ** for great, *** for outstanding.
4. Create the Fact Sheet: exhaustive detail about the product (weight, dimensions, author bio, specifications, history).
5. Create the Benefit List: translate every fact into a buyer benefit. Aim for several dozen.
6. Create the Deal Sheet: write the strongest possible offer in a few lines. Think more about sweetening this offer than any other aspect. Ask: "What would make saying 'no' feel stupid?"
7. Pull out your swipe file and re-read the best ads and sales letters. Copy winning headlines onto 3x5 index cards.
8. STOP. Walk away for a day or two. Let the subconscious work.
9. When the breakthrough idea ("aha experience") arrives -- a grabber concept, a new angle, a visualization technique -- write it down immediately.
10. Write the first draft following AIDA. Start with the central selling idea in the first sentence (letters) or headline (ads).
11. Write fast. Get everything out: every detail, every benefit, every nuance. "Write. Write. Write. Rave! Rave! Rave! Don't worry about getting it perfect."
12. Set aside for a day. Let it cool.
13. Rework in AIDA sequence: (1) attention-getting opening, (2) expand central selling idea with interesting facts, (3) describe benefits so reader pictures ownership with pleasure, (4) extremely specific call to action.
14. Apply the "so what" test to every claim.
15. Format for eye relief: wide margins, short paragraphs, subheads, bold, parenthetical asides.

**Technique 3: Writing Bullets/Fascinations**

1. Take a fact about the product (e.g., "Chapter 7 covers how to negotiate with car dealers").
2. "Twist" it into a tease that hints at the benefit without revealing it: "The one question you must ask any car dealer that will instantly slash $2,000 off the sticker price -- most buyers are too embarrassed to ask it!"
3. Rules: must be 100% truthful, must create curiosity that can only be satisfied by purchasing, must be specific (use exact numbers, not vague claims).
4. Write dozens. A reader may order because of a single bullet that resonates.

**Technique 4: The "Life or Death" Test for Any Mailing**

Before finalizing any direct mail piece, ask: "If I would literally be killed if this mailing failed to get a response, would I send it exactly like this?" If any element fails this test (bulk rate postage, label addressing, teaser copy, visible brochures), change it.

**Technique 5: George Zangas Postcard Method**

Halbert wrote a postcard for world-famous power lifter George Zangas that generated $1,000 for every $100 spent -- and continued to profit even when mailed to the same list five different times. The key elements: (1) a specific, benefit-driven message, (2) a "free recorded message" call-to-action (phone number with 24-hour recorded message that pre-sold the offer), (3) simple black-and-white design (stands out when everything else uses color), (4) mailed to a proven buyer list with high desire for the product category.
