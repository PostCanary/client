## Aaron Draplin -- Initial Scan

### Who They Are

Aaron James Draplin (born 1973, Detroit, Michigan) is an American graphic designer, entrepreneur, and author based in Portland, Oregon. He runs the one-man Draplin Design Co. (DDC), founded in 2004. Co-founder of Field Notes brand. Clients include Ford Motors, Nike, Patagonia, Burton Snowboards, Esquire, Red Wing, the Obama Administration, and Target. Designed a US postal stamp (Star Ribbon, 2019). Former art director of Snowboarder Magazine. Hugely popular speaker and Skillshare instructor. Known for a larger-than-life personality and blue-collar work ethic despite working with Fortune 500 brands.

### Key Works

- **Draplin Design Co.: Pretty Much Everything** (2016, Abrams Books) -- 256-page mid-career survey. Case studies, process, how-tos, road stories. 5 printings, 33,000+ copies. The primary written source for his design philosophy.
- **Field Notes** -- Co-founded line of memo notebooks. "New nostalgia" product design rooted in vintage Americana. His most commercially successful product venture.
- **"Thick Lines" series** -- Personal project exploring reductionist vector illustration with uniform thick outlines. Became his signature style identifier.
- **Things We Love "State" Posters** -- Series of state-themed posters.
- **Skillshare classes** (8 total) -- Covers logo design, type customization, merch design, iteration, color, workflow speed, business of design. Most accessible source for his actual process and rules.
- **TEDxPortland talk** and hundreds of speaking gigs ("Tall Tales from a Large Man").

### Core Idea (one paragraph)

Draplin's design philosophy centers on functional reductionism rooted in mid-century American commercial art. His concrete principles: (1) Scalability above all -- a logo must work at the size of a pea and the size of a building; (2) Thick, consistent line weights create visual uniformity and a "Utopian feel"; (3) Restraint as refinement -- "the practice of learning how to edit back and only deliver the essential information"; (4) Reproducibility across physical media -- designs must work on a hat, a van, a water bottle, an envelope, not just a screen; (5) Authenticity over trend-chasing -- "credibility and originality come from authenticity," not from following what's en vogue; (6) Hierarchy, line-weight, and graphic balance are the fundamental tools, not effects or decoration. He explicitly traces his lineage to Saul Bass, Paul Rand, Seymour Chwast, Milton Glaser, and Keith Haring -- calling thick lines and bold simplicity "not a style" but "basic principles of graphic design" that worked in 1966 and 2016 alike. His anti-principle is equally clear: no complexity for complexity's sake, no convoluted bells and whistles, no gradients-and-shadows approach. "I'm just clearing out the bullshit."

### Initial Red Flags

1. **Practitioner, not theorist.** Draplin explicitly resists articulating formal rules or systems. His principles emerge from interviews and talks, not from structured frameworks. You have to extract the rules from stories and asides -- he won't hand you a numbered list of "here's how to make print look real." This means any rules derived from him require interpretation.

2. **One-trick-pony criticism is real and acknowledged.** He calls himself "a 13-trick pony" defensively. Multiple interviewers and commenters push back on whether his thick-lines-and-bold-type approach is actually versatile. His rebuttal is that people only see his personal projects, not his client work -- but his most visible, influential work IS the thick-lines stuff. For PostCanary's purposes this is fine (we want exactly that aesthetic), but be aware we'd be borrowing from his most narrow band.

3. **Print-first, web-skeptical.** Draplin is openly frustrated with web design and admits he can't build websites. His quote: "Can I build a website? And I can't." His design rules are forged for physical media -- stickers, patches, notebooks, posters, van wraps. Translating his principles to a digital product (even one that produces print output like PostCanary) requires bridging work he hasn't done himself.

4. **Personality-driven, not methodology-driven.** A huge amount of his influence comes from charisma, speaking presence, and personal brand rather than from transferable methodology. The risk is adopting "vibes" rather than extractable rules.

5. **No formal writing on print vs. digital distinction.** He lives in print and avoids digital, but hasn't written a systematic comparison of what makes print design look "real" vs. what makes digital design look amateur. We'd need to infer those rules from his work and scattered comments.

### Relevance Assessment

**Relevance: HIGH -- with caveats.**

Draplin is the single most prominent living practitioner of the exact aesthetic PostCanary needs: bold, confident, thick-lined, high-contrast, physically-rooted American graphic design. His extractable principles align almost perfectly with what we're looking for:

- Thick consistent line weights (not thin hairlines, not variable-weight trendy strokes)
- Scalability as the primary test of good design (works at stamp size and billboard size)
- Restraint and reductionism over decoration
- Functional first, fashionable second
- Color blocks and solid shapes over gradients, shadows, and soft effects
- Designs that feel like they belong on physical objects (patches, stickers, notebooks, signage)
- Explicit lineage to Saul Bass / Paul Rand / mid-century commercial art

**Caveats for PostCanary use:**

1. He won't give us a formal system -- we'll need to extract and codify rules from his scattered interviews and the "Pretty Much Everything" book.
2. His web/digital skepticism means we need to do the translation work ourselves: how do his print principles map to a digital tool that produces physical postcards?
3. His aesthetic is strongest for logos, icons, and simple compositions. PostCanary postcards involve more complex layouts (text + imagery + branding). We may need to supplement with someone who works at the layout/composition level, not just the mark-making level.
4. The "one-trick pony" criticism suggests his range has limits. For the specific PostCanary use case (bold, confident, "looks like a real business sent this"), those limits work in our favor -- we want exactly that one trick.

**Recommendation:** Proceed to deep dive. Best ROI sources are (1) the Design Week thick-lines excerpt, (2) the Raised by Wolves interview for print-vs-digital contrast, (3) the Skillshare Blog synthesis for codifiable principles, and (4) purchasing/reading Pretty Much Everything for the complete philosophy.
