## Heath Brothers -- Initial Scan

### Who They Are

- **Chip Heath**: Professor of Organizational Behavior at Stanford Graduate School of Business. Teaches business strategy and organizations courses. His academic research focuses on why certain ideas survive and spread.
- **Dan Heath**: Senior Fellow at Duke University's CASE center (supporting social entrepreneurs). Former Harvard Business School researcher. Co-founded an innovative publishing/education company (Thinkwell) exploring new forms of textbook delivery.
- They are brothers who independently converged on the same research question -- why some ideas stick while others fail -- and then collaborated.
- They also wrote a regular column for Fast Company magazine.

### Key Works

1. **Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die** (2007, Random House) -- the SUCCESs framework. NYT and WSJ bestseller, 24-month BusinessWeek run, translated into 29+ languages. Named one of the 100 best business books of all time.
2. **Switch: How to Change Things When Change Is Hard** (2010) -- behavior change framework (Rider/Elephant/Path).
3. **Decisive: How to Make Better Decisions in Life and Work** (2013) -- decision-making framework (WRAP process).
4. **The Power of Moments: Why Certain Experiences Stand Out** (2017) -- designing peak experiences (Elevation/Insight/Pride/Connection).
5. **Upstream: How to Solve Problems Before They Happen** (2020) -- systemic/preventive thinking.
6. **Reset: How to Change What's Not Working** (2025, Dan Heath solo) -- organizational change.

### Core Idea (one paragraph)

The SUCCESs framework proposes that sticky ideas -- those that are understood, remembered, and change behavior -- share six common traits: **Simple** (strip to the essential core, not five priorities but one), **Unexpected** (break patterns to grab attention, then open curiosity gaps to hold it), **Concrete** (use sensory language and tangible examples instead of abstractions), **Credible** (build believability through testable credentials, vivid details, statistics made human-scale, or the "Sinatra Test"), **Emotional** (connect to identity and values to make people care, not just comprehend), and **Stories** (provide mental simulation -- "flight simulators for behavior" -- so people can rehearse action). The framework is explicitly designed as a checklist: the book itself includes a grid where you score a message against each dimension (check or dash). The primary antagonist is the "Curse of Knowledge" -- experts cannot un-know what they know, so they communicate in abstractions that slide off their audience.

### Initial Red Flags

**For our PostCanary use case specifically:**

1. **The framework was designed for verbal/written ideas, not visual media.** Every example in the book is a slogan, campaign narrative, urban legend, or spoken message. There is zero treatment of how SUCCESs applies to a visual artifact like a postcard where the recipient has under 2 seconds of attention. We would need to adapt it ourselves.

2. **No scoring rubric exists.** The book's "checklist" is binary (check/dash per dimension) with no gradation. The book explicitly says "this is not a mathematical formula" and "not all ideas need all six." Converting this into a quantitative scoring system for automated postcard evaluation would be our own invention, not theirs.

3. **"Stories" dimension is problematic for postcards.** A postcard cannot tell a story in any traditional sense. This dimension may need to be reinterpreted (e.g., "does the postcard imply a narrative?") or dropped, which weakens the framework as a complete checklist.

4. **"Credible" dimension is weak for the postcard context.** Postcard credibility comes from brand recognition, review stars, and trust signals -- not from the rhetorical techniques Heath describes (testable credentials, Sinatra test). Again, adaptation needed.

5. **Dated examples and some superficial analysis.** Multiple reviewers note the examples feel dated (Jared/Subway is now toxic, no social media awareness). A critical review (jomrcr.svbtle.com) argues the book confuses cleverness with principled application -- the examples worked because the people were clever, not because the framework was applied mechanically. This is a real concern if we're trying to build an automated scorer.

6. **No empirical validation of the framework itself.** The book cites psychology research (tapping study, memory research, etc.) to support individual principles, but there is no study that validates SUCCESs as a complete, predictive system. The Israeli ad-template study they cite showed that trained people produced better ads, but the training used six creative templates, not the SUCCESs checklist specifically.

7. **The "Curse of Knowledge" insight is more valuable than the checklist.** For PostCanary, the real takeaway may be: business owners writing their own postcard copy suffer from the Curse of Knowledge (they know their business too well to see it from the customer's perspective). The checklist is a tool to counteract that curse -- but it's a blunt tool.

### Relevance Assessment

**Overall relevance to PostCanary postcard scoring: MEDIUM-HIGH with significant adaptation required.**

**What transfers directly:**
- The six dimensions are a legitimate, well-established lens for evaluating whether a message will be remembered and acted upon. The framework has been used by marketers, teachers, and communicators for 19 years.
- The "Simple" dimension maps perfectly to postcard design: does the card communicate one core message, or is it cluttered with five offers?
- The "Unexpected" dimension is highly relevant: does the postcard break the pattern of junk mail enough to earn a second look?
- The "Concrete" dimension maps well: does the postcard use specific, tangible language ("save $200 on your next AC repair") vs. vague abstractions ("quality service you can trust")?
- The "Emotional" dimension is relevant: does the postcard connect to what the recipient actually cares about?

**What does NOT transfer:**
- "Stories" -- a postcard cannot tell a story. This dimension needs reinterpretation or replacement.
- "Credible" -- postcard credibility works differently (Google stars, "Licensed & Insured", before/after photos) than rhetorical credibility.
- The framework has no visual dimension at all. Postcard stickiness depends heavily on visual hierarchy, color contrast, image quality, and layout -- none of which SUCCESs addresses.
- No scoring granularity. Binary pass/fail per dimension is too coarse for a product that needs to produce a meaningful score.

**Recommendation:** The SUCCESs framework is a strong conceptual foundation for the "message effectiveness" component of a postcard score, but it cannot be the sole scoring system. It needs to be (a) adapted for visual media, (b) combined with design/visual scoring dimensions it does not cover, and (c) converted from a binary checklist to a graduated rubric -- all of which would be PostCanary's original contribution, not the Heaths' work.

**Next step if pursuing:** Read the actual "Easy Reference Guide" from the book (p. 253+) to see if there are more granular pass/fail criteria per dimension than what the summaries capture. The downloadable "Made to Stick Model" from danheath.com may also contain a more detailed scoring reference.
