## Heath Brothers -- Deep Research

### Origin Story

Chip Heath (Stanford professor of Organizational Behavior) and Dan Heath (Duke CASE center fellow, former education startup founder) independently spent a decade studying the same question from opposite angles: why do some ideas survive and spread while others die? Chip analyzed "naturally sticky" ideas -- urban legends, proverbs, conspiracy theories, wartime rumors -- coding hundreds of examples to identify shared traits. Dan built Thinkwell, a video-textbook company, working with the nation's best teachers to figure out what made lessons stick. In 2004 they realized they'd converged on the same six principles. The result was *Made to Stick* (2007), which became a NYT/WSJ bestseller, translated into 29+ languages, and named one of the 100 best business books of all time.

The core villain in their framework is the **Curse of Knowledge** -- once you know something, you cannot imagine not knowing it. This is why experts communicate in abstractions that slide off their audience. The tapper/listener experiment (Elizabeth Newton, Stanford 1990) is their foundational demonstration: tappers predicted listeners would guess songs 50% of the time; actual success rate was 2.5%. The entire SUCCESs framework is a weapon against this curse.

### Core Methodology (the 6 SUCCESs dimensions -- each with actionable definition)

**1. SIMPLE -- Find the core, then express it compactly.**
Strip the idea to its most critical essence through relentless prioritization. "If you argue ten points, even if each is a good point, when they get back to the jury room they won't remember any." The goal is NOT brevity (sound bites) but profundity (proverbs). The Golden Rule is the model: one sentence so deep you could spend a lifetime on it. Use "schema" -- anchor new ideas to things people already know (pomelo = "a big grapefruit"). The Commander's Intent model: one crisp, plain-talk statement of the desired end-state.

**2. UNEXPECTED -- Violate expectations to grab attention, then open curiosity gaps to hold it.**
Surprise is an emotion whose biological function is to increase alertness and focus. But surprise is momentary. To sustain attention, you must open "knowledge gaps" -- create a mystery, pose a question, hint at answers but withhold the resolution. "The 'huh?' moment prepares the scene for the 'aha!' moment." The Israeli ad study found that 89% of award-winning ads fit six templates, most involving unexpectedness (e.g., "extreme consequences" template). Only 2% of unsuccessful ads fit any template.

**3. CONCRETE -- Use sensory, specific language so the idea means the same thing to everyone.**
Abstract ideas ("maximize shareholder value") are ambiguous to the point of meaninglessness. Sticky ideas are full of concrete images: ice-filled bathtubs, apples with razors, a table of greasy food. "Our brains are wired to remember concrete data." In proverbs, abstract truths are encoded in concrete language: "A bird in hand is worth two in the bush." The test: can a person SEE, TOUCH, TASTE, or HEAR the idea? If not, it's too abstract.

**4. CREDIBLE -- Give people a way to test the idea for themselves.**
Most of us lack authority credibility (we're not the Surgeon General). Sticky ideas carry their own credentials through: (a) vivid, specific details that serve as a proxy for expertise, (b) statistics made human-scale and relatable rather than raw numbers, (c) testable claims ("Before you vote, ask yourself if you are better off today than four years ago" -- Reagan), (d) anti-authority (the guy who wore 60-inch pants giving diet advice = the Pam Laffin / "Sinatra Test" credibility), (e) the "try before you buy" philosophy for ideas.

**5. EMOTIONAL -- Make people feel something so they care enough to act.**
"People are more likely to make a charitable gift to a single needy individual than to an entire impoverished region." We are wired to feel for people, not abstractions (the Mother Teresa Effect). The hard part is finding the RIGHT emotion. Fear doesn't work on teenagers for anti-smoking; resentment of Big Tobacco's duplicity does. Connect to identity and self-interest on Maslow's hierarchy -- not just base needs, but belonging, esteem, self-actualization. Appeal to who people ARE or want to BECOME, not just what they need.

**6. STORIES -- Provide mental flight simulators so people can rehearse action.**
"Stories are like flight simulators for the brain." Firefighters swap stories after every fire, multiplying their experience. Hearing stories prepares us to respond more quickly in real situations. Three archetypal plots: (a) Challenge plot -- protagonist overcomes obstacles (inspires action), (b) Connection plot -- people who bridge a gap (inspires relationships), (c) Creativity plot -- someone solves a problem in a novel way (inspires new thinking). You don't have to invent stories -- you have to get good at spotting them.

### Decision Rules (MOST IMPORTANT -- if/then rules for scoring each dimension on a postcard)

**NOTE: The Heath brothers explicitly state "There is no 'formula' for a sticky idea" and the book's checklist is binary (check/dash per dimension) with no graduated scoring. The following decision rules are DERIVED from their principles and examples, adapted for postcard evaluation. These are PostCanary's interpretation, not the Heaths' own scoring system.**

**SIMPLE**
- If the postcard communicates ONE clear offer/message that a 10-year-old could repeat back, then PASS. If it tries to communicate 3+ offers, services, or messages simultaneously, then FAIL because "if you argue ten points... they won't remember any."
- If the core message can be understood without reading body copy (headline + image alone), then PASS. If the recipient must read fine print to understand the offer, then FAIL because the Commander's Intent must be visible at a glance.
- If the postcard uses a familiar comparison/schema to explain something new (e.g., "like Uber for home repairs"), then PASS. If it uses jargon or industry-specific language, then FAIL because the Curse of Knowledge is at work.

**UNEXPECTED**
- If the postcard breaks the visual or verbal pattern of typical junk mail (unexpected image, counterintuitive claim, surprising statistic), then PASS. If it looks like every other postcard in the mailbox (stock photo + generic headline), then FAIL because it will not trigger the surprise response that causes focus.
- If the postcard creates a curiosity gap (a question, a mystery, an incomplete story that makes you flip the card), then PASS. If the entire message is fully resolved on the front, then NEUTRAL -- surprise alone can suffice for a postcard's 2-second window.
- If the headline violates a schema (e.g., "Your AC is making you sick" for an HVAC company), then PASS. If the headline confirms what the reader already assumes ("Quality HVAC Service"), then FAIL because no schema violation = no attention grab.

**CONCRETE**
- If the postcard uses specific, tangible language with numbers ("Save $200 on your next AC repair," "Served 4,127 homes in [ZIP code]"), then PASS. If it uses vague abstractions ("quality service you can trust," "excellence in every detail"), then FAIL because abstract language "is ambiguous to the point of being meaningless."
- If the postcard includes a sensory element the recipient can visualize (before/after photo, a specific scenario, a tangible object), then PASS. If the imagery is generic stock photography with no connection to the specific offer, then FAIL because concreteness requires sensory information.
- If the offer has specific terms (dollar amount, percentage, deadline, scope), then PASS. If the offer is vague ("call for a free estimate"), then PARTIAL PASS -- it's concrete enough to act on but not memorable.

**CREDIBLE**
- If the postcard includes a testable proof element (Google star rating with review count, specific number of jobs completed, "Licensed & Bonded" with license number, before/after photos of real work), then PASS. If there are no trust signals beyond the business's own claims, then FAIL because the postcard must "carry its own credentials."
- If statistics are human-scale and relatable ("We've fixed 300 roofs on your street" vs. "10,000 jobs completed nationwide"), then PASS. If raw numbers are presented without context, then PARTIAL PASS because numbers without human scale don't register.
- If the postcard uses "anti-authority" credibility (a real customer testimonial, a relatable person rather than a corporate logo), then PASS. If it relies solely on corporate branding with no social proof, then FAIL for local service businesses.

**EMOTIONAL**
- If the postcard connects to what the recipient actually cares about (their home, their family's comfort, saving money, avoiding hassle), then PASS. If it talks about what the BUSINESS cares about ("We're proud of our 20 years of service"), then FAIL because "people care about people" -- appeal to the recipient's identity, not the sender's pride.
- If the postcard taps into a specific emotion (relief from a problem, fear of a risk, excitement about saving money, pride in their home), then PASS. If the tone is neutral/informational with no emotional trigger, then FAIL because without emotion people comprehend but don't care enough to act.
- If the message targets a single person rather than a mass ("Your roof" not "homeowners"), then PASS. If it addresses an abstract group, then FAIL because the Mother Teresa Effect shows we feel for individuals, not populations.

**STORIES**
- If the postcard implies a narrative arc (a before/after transformation, a customer journey, a problem-to-solution sequence), then PASS. If the postcard is purely declarative ("We do X, call us"), then FAIL for this dimension -- but NOTE: the Heaths acknowledge not all sticky ideas need all six traits.
- If the postcard features a real customer or scenario that lets the recipient mentally simulate their own experience, then PASS. If it's entirely abstract/corporate, then FAIL because stories function as "mental flight simulators."
- **IMPORTANT CAVEAT FOR POSTCARDS:** A postcard cannot tell a full story. This dimension should be weighted lower than the other five for postcard evaluation, or reinterpreted as "does the postcard suggest a narrative the recipient can complete in their mind?"

### Specific Techniques (how to make a message pass each dimension)

**Making it SIMPLE:**
1. Use the "inverted pyramid" -- lead with the most important thing, not the background.
2. Apply the "Commander's Intent" test: if everything else gets stripped away, what's the ONE thing that must survive?
3. Use analogies and schemas to compress complex ideas ("It's like X but for Y").
4. Count the number of distinct messages. If > 1, cut until you have one.

**Making it UNEXPECTED:**
1. Identify the central counterintuitive element of your message. What assumption does it violate?
2. Use the "Extreme Consequences" template -- exaggerate the benefit to make it vivid.
3. Open a curiosity gap: start with a question or mystery rather than a declarative statement.
4. Find the "uncommon sense" angle -- what's true about your offering that contradicts common belief?

**Making it CONCRETE:**
1. Replace every abstract noun with a sensory equivalent. "Quality" becomes "a 5-star Google rating from 847 homeowners."
2. Use specific numbers: dollar amounts, percentages, quantities, time frames.
3. Include a "tangible anchor" -- a physical object, photo, or scenario the audience can picture.
4. Apply the "show, don't tell" test. Could you photograph this claim? If not, make it more concrete.

**Making it CREDIBLE:**
1. Include testable claims the recipient can verify ("Check our 4.9 stars on Google").
2. Make statistics human-scale: "That's enough to fill your living room" rather than "5,000 cubic feet."
3. Use the anti-authority: real customers, real results, real stories rather than corporate assertions.
4. Deploy the Sinatra Test: one overwhelming proof point that makes everything else believable.

**Making it EMOTIONAL:**
1. Put a face on the message -- use a single person, not a group or statistic.
2. Appeal to identity: "You're the kind of homeowner who..." rather than "Homeowners should..."
3. Find the right emotion -- not always positive. Fear, disgust, pride, relief can all drive action.
4. Connect to Maslow's upper levels: belonging, esteem, self-actualization, not just price/convenience.

**Making it a STORY (adapted for postcards):**
1. Use a before/after structure -- the simplest story arc that fits a postcard format.
2. Feature a real customer with a real result (mini-testimonial as micro-story).
3. Imply a narrative the recipient completes: "Last winter, the Johnsons' furnace died at 2am..." (they imagine the rest).
4. Use the Challenge Plot: show someone overcoming a problem the recipient relates to.

### Quotes That Reveal Their Thinking (2-3 direct quotes with sources)

**1. On the nature of stickiness:**
> "There is no 'formula' for a sticky idea -- we don't want to overstate the case. But sticky ideas do draw from a common set of traits, which make them more likely to succeed."
> -- *Made to Stick*, Introduction (heathbrothers.com/made-to-stick-introduction/)

This quote is critical because it shows the framework is probabilistic, not deterministic. A postcard scoring system built on SUCCESs should increase the likelihood of effectiveness, not guarantee it.

**2. On the enemy of all communication:**
> "When a tapper taps, she is hearing the song in her head... Meanwhile, the listeners can't hear that tune -- all they can hear is a bunch of disconnected taps. This is the Curse of Knowledge. Once we know something, we find it hard to imagine what it was like not to know it."
> -- *Made to Stick*, Introduction (tapper/listener experiment description)

This is the foundational insight for PostCanary: every business owner writing postcard copy is a tapper. They hear the full symphony of their business -- their 20 years of experience, their craftsmanship, their customer relationships. The recipient hears disconnected taps. The SUCCESs checklist forces the owner to translate from tapper to listener.

**3. On the power of systematic creativity over innate genius:**
> "Highly creative ads are more predictable than uncreative ones. It's like Tolstoy's quote: 'All happy families resemble each other, but each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way.' All creative ads resemble one another, but each loser is uncreative in its own way."
> -- *Made to Stick*, Introduction (discussing the Israeli ad template research)

This validates the entire concept of a scoring checklist. If effective messages are systematically similar (they follow templates/patterns), then a rubric that detects those patterns can reliably distinguish effective from ineffective postcards.
