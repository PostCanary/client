## Heath Brothers — Critics & Limitations

### What Critics Say

**1. The framework is amoral — it works equally well for lies (Barry Schwartz, Washington Post, 2007)**
Swarthmore psychology professor Barry Schwartz wrote the most substantive published critique. He argues SUCCESs is missing three critical qualities he abbreviates as WIT: ideas should be **Worthwhile**, **Important**, and **True** (which is not the same as credible). The Heaths' own starting point — urban legends — proves the problem: urban legends are maximally sticky and entirely false. Schwartz warns that "when everyone around you is applying SUCCES, you will have to exaggerate, distort or even lie to be noticed." The framework is a weapon with no safety. It describes what makes ideas *memorable*, not what makes them *good*.

**2. The examples confuse cleverness with the principles (jomrcr, critical review, 2014)**
The most detailed negative review argues that the book's examples don't actually validate the framework — they validate the cleverness of the people who created the messages. "Was the teacher's message sticky because of the concreteness, or because of the cleverness?" If the principles only work when applied by unusually talented communicators, then the framework is not transferable to average users — which is exactly our PostCanary use case (average business owners writing postcard copy).

**3. The book is hypocritical — it violates its own rules (jomrcr)**
The book claims to "strip down to its core" but then presents six principles inside two steps, violating its own simplicity doctrine. The Southwest Airlines example ("THE low-fare airline") is offered as the model of simplicity, but Southwest serves drinks and offers perks that contradict this positioning. The reviewer finds these contradictions consistently: "Hypocrisy also runs abound in the examples this book cites, which are often contradictory when analyzed at anything more than a cursory depth."

**4. "Relentlessly breezy and populist style" raises credibility concerns (Elizabeth Wager, BMJ / Journal of the Royal Society of Medicine, 2007)**
Medical communication researcher Wager applied SUCCESs to evidence-based medicine and found an inherent tension: systematic reviews — the gold standard of medical evidence — fail virtually every SUCCESs test (they are complex, expected, abstract, unemotional, and contain no stories). Meanwhile, individual case studies pass every test but represent the weakest form of evidence. The framework may systematically favor anecdote over evidence.

**5. The reviewer's own SUCCESs framework didn't stick (Mark Ward, By Faith We Understand, 2016)**
A reviewer who deliberately let the book "marinate" for a week found that the SUCCESs acronym itself didn't fully stick — he had to look it up again. He also noted that the framework "will feel its age more quickly than, say, the persuasive modes of Aristotle (logos, pathos, and ethos)," which have served a similar function for 2,400 years.

**6. "Argument by anecdote" with pop social science foundation (multiple reviewers)**
Several reviewers note the Gladwellian structure: compelling stories presented as evidence, with selective citation of psychology studies. This is not the same as rigorous empirical validation of the framework as a whole.

### When This Methodology Fails

**1. Visual media and non-verbal communication.**
Every example in the book is a verbal/written message: slogans, speeches, campaign narratives, urban legends. There is zero treatment of visual communication — layout, color, typography, image selection, visual hierarchy. A postcard, billboard, or Instagram ad operates under different cognitive rules (pre-attentive processing, peripheral vision, visual salience) that SUCCESs does not address. Dan Heath himself critiqued the SlideShare "World's Best Presentation" winners, noting that "stunning visuals aren't used in the service of a sticky idea" — but he offered no visual framework to replace it.

**2. Time-constrained contexts (under 2 seconds).**
SUCCESs was developed for ideas that get at least moderate attention: classroom lessons, campaign speeches, news stories, ad campaigns. A postcard in a mailbox gets under 2 seconds of attention. The framework has no mechanism for the split-second "keep or trash" decision that governs physical mail. "Unexpected" may be the only dimension that operates at this timescale.

**3. Complex, nuanced, or probabilistic ideas.**
The medical communication review (Wager, 2007) demonstrates this clearly: systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and evidence-based guidelines are inherently non-sticky by SUCCESs standards. The framework actively penalizes complexity and nuance. For products or services that require nuanced explanation, forcing simplicity may distort the message.

**4. Expert-to-expert communication.**
The jomrcr reviewer argues that the book's advice to make things "concrete" is sometimes just advice to "dumb things down." When engineers communicate with manufacturing staff via concrete demonstrations instead of abstract blueprints, the book calls it a success — but the reviewer calls it inefficient. The framework assumes a general audience and may be counterproductive for specialist communication.

**5. When everyone applies it simultaneously.**
Schwartz's "arms race" argument: if everyone's messages are Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, and Story-based, then no message stands out. The framework may be self-defeating at scale. "Unusual just isn't unusual enough to cut it anymore."

**6. When truth, importance, and ethics matter more than memorability.**
The framework cannot distinguish between a sticky true idea and a sticky false one. Anti-vaccine messaging, conspiracy theories, and propaganda score high on SUCCESs. The framework describes viral mechanics, not message quality.

**7. Stories dimension on space-constrained formats.**
A postcard, tweet, or text message cannot tell a story. The "Stories" dimension requires narrative arc — setup, conflict, resolution — that simply does not fit in many modern communication formats. The Heaths acknowledge "not all ideas need all six" but provide no guidance on which dimensions to drop or how to weight them.

### What They've Been Wrong About

**1. The Israeli ad template study is misrepresented.**
The Heaths cite a study showing 89% of award-winning ads fit six creative templates. They use this to validate SUCCESs. But the six templates in that study (Goldenberg, Mazursky & Solomon, 1999) are NOT the six SUCCESs principles — they are specific creative patterns (pictorial analogy, extreme situations, consequences, competition, interactive experiment, dimensionality alteration). The leap from "creative templates predict ad success" to "SUCCESs principles predict idea stickiness" is the Heaths' own inference, not the study's finding.

**2. The Jared/Subway example aged catastrophically.**
Jared Fogle, held up as a model of sticky storytelling, was later convicted of sex crimes involving minors. Multiple reviewers note this makes the example "toxic." More importantly, it reveals a survivorship bias problem: the Heaths selected examples that were successful *at the time of writing* without considering durability. The stickiest ideas are not always the best ideas.

**3. No prediction, only post-hoc explanation.**
The framework is applied retrospectively to explain why successful ideas succeeded. There is no published evidence that applying SUCCESs prospectively (before creating a message) reliably produces stickier messages than not applying it. The one academic study that tested this (Liu et al., 2022, Western Journal of Communication) found that sticky messages did decay less than non-sticky messages over time, but the authors noted "its explanation remains elusive" — the mechanism was unclear even when the effect appeared.

**4. The "Curse of Knowledge" may be more useful than the checklist.**
Several reviewers and the scan analysis agree: the Curse of Knowledge insight (experts cannot un-know what they know, so they communicate in abstractions) is the book's most durable contribution. The SUCCESs checklist is a tool to counteract that curse, but it may be a blunt tool relative to the insight itself.

### Key Debates

**1. Is stickiness the right goal?**
Schwartz's WIT critique raises a fundamental question: should we optimize for memorability, or for truth and importance? The framework conflates "people remember this" with "this is effective communication." But remembered misinformation is worse than forgotten truth.

**2. Descriptive vs. prescriptive validity.**
The framework clearly describes common traits of ideas that have already stuck. Whether it works as a prescriptive tool — apply these six principles and your message will stick — is unproven. The jomrcr reviewer argues the examples prove cleverness more than they prove the principles.

**3. Does simplicity require distortion?**
The "Simple" principle demands stripping to one core message. But many important ideas (climate change, public health policy, financial planning) are irreducibly complex. The jomrcr reviewer and the BMJ reviewer both argue that enforced simplicity may distort messages in dangerous ways.

**4. Aristotle got there first (and better?).**
Multiple reviewers note that logos (logic/credibility), pathos (emotion), and ethos (character/authority) cover much of the same ground as SUCCESs, with 2,400 years of rhetorical validation. The Heath contribution may be more accessible but less durable than classical rhetoric.

### Survivorship Bias Check

**Severe survivorship bias present.** The entire methodology is built on analyzing ideas that already succeeded. The Heaths studied urban legends that spread, campaigns that worked, slogans that stuck. They did not systematically study:

- Ideas that had all six SUCCESs traits but failed anyway
- Ideas that lacked SUCCESs traits but succeeded through other mechanisms (repetition, distribution power, social pressure, institutional mandate)
- The base rate of success for SUCCESs-compliant vs. non-compliant messages
- Ideas that were sticky but harmful (though they acknowledge urban legends, they don't treat this as a framework flaw)

The book's method is inherently retrospective pattern-matching on winners. This is the same methodological flaw Jim Collins' *Built to Last* and *Good to Great* were later criticized for — the comparison companies often declined after publication.

The Jared/Subway example is the clearest survivorship casualty: selected as a winner, later revealed as catastrophically wrong.

### Updated Thinking

**Limited evolution since 2007.** The Heaths have not published a revised edition of *Made to Stick* that addresses these criticisms. Their subsequent books (*Switch*, *Decisive*, *The Power of Moments*, *Upstream*) address adjacent topics but do not revisit or update the SUCCESs framework.

**One academic validation attempt exists.** Liu et al. (2022) tested sticky vs. non-sticky messages in a longitudinal experiment on sunscreen use. Results showed sticky messages decayed less over time, providing partial support. But the authors found the mechanism "elusive" and needed a second experiment to replicate with a potential explanation. This is thin validation for a framework cited thousands of times.

**The JMR editorial (2008) applied it to academic writing** but explicitly concluded "Is stickiness sufficient? In a word, no." The editor noted that substance must precede style.

**No known revision for digital/visual/social media contexts.** The framework was designed for a pre-social-media world of speeches, print ads, and broadcast campaigns. No published adaptation addresses Instagram, TikTok, direct mail design, or any visual-first medium. Any such adaptation (including what PostCanary would build) is original work, not the Heaths' methodology.

**Bottom line for PostCanary:** The SUCCESs framework provides a legitimate conceptual vocabulary for *message* evaluation (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional) but cannot serve as a complete postcard scoring system. It has no visual dimension, no empirical scoring rubric, no validation for space-constrained or time-pressured formats, and significant survivorship bias in its evidence base. The "Stories" dimension is inapplicable to postcards. Any scoring system built on it would be PostCanary's original invention using Heath vocabulary, not a validated Heath methodology.
