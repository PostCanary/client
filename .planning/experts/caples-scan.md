## John Caples -- Initial Scan

### Who They Are

John Caples (1900-1990) was an American advertising copywriter and direct response pioneer who spent 49 years at BBDO, eventually becoming vice president. He started his advertising career in 1925 after graduating from the U.S. Naval Academy. In 1926, at age 26, he wrote what is widely considered the most famous advertisement headline in history: "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano But When I Started to Play!" for the U.S. School of Music. He was inducted into the Copywriters Hall of Fame (1973) and the American Advertising Federation Hall of Fame (1977-1978). David Ogilvy called him "one of the most effective copywriters who has ever lived" and "an indomitable analyzer and teacher of advertising."

### Key Works

- **Tested Advertising Methods** (1932, 5 editions through 1998) -- his magnum opus, the primary source for headline formulas. 5th edition updated by Fred E. Hahn. 4.23/5 on Goodreads (1,197 ratings). Five of its 18 chapters are dedicated to headlines.
- **Advertising for Immediate Sales** (1936)
- **Advertising Ideas: A Practical Guide** (1938)
- **Making Ads Pay: Timeless Tips for Successful Copywriting** (1957)
- **How to Make Your Advertising Make Money** (1983)
- **A Dozen Ways to Develop Advertising Ideas** (1984, essay)

### Core Idea (one paragraph)

Caples was the original "test everything" copywriter -- he treated advertising as an empirical science, not an art. His central thesis: the headline is the most important element of any advertisement (responsible for up to 75% of an ad's impact), and headlines can be systematically categorized and tested. He identified four qualities of effective headlines ranked by power: (1) Self-interest (benefits to the reader -- the strongest), (2) News (new products, new uses, announcements), (3) Curiosity (intrigue that hooks -- but only effective when combined with self-interest or news), and (4) Quick/Easy Way (suggesting a fast path to something the reader wants). From decades of mail-order split testing (where every response was trackable), he distilled 35 specific headline formulas organized into five categories, plus principles for body copy, appeals, and testing methodology. His approach was radically empirical for the era: run the same ad with different headlines and different return addresses, then count which address got more responses.

### Initial Red Flags

- **Era gap**: His testing was done in print media (newspapers, magazines, direct mail) from the 1920s through the 1980s. No digital, no social media, no subject lines, no push notifications. The formulas may need translation for modern contexts.
- **No criticism sources found**: Search #3 for criticism/limitations returned zero critical academic analysis. Every source found is either hagiographic or neutral summary. This is a yellow flag -- it may mean the work is genuinely foundational and beyond reproach, or it may mean nobody has bothered to critically examine a dead man's legacy.
- **5th edition dilution**: The 1998 edition was updated by Fred E. Hahn after Caples's death (1990). Some reviewers note the updates may have altered the original voice. The 4th edition (1974) or earlier may be more authentic to Caples's own thinking.
- **Mail-order specificity**: His testing methodology was rigorous but specific to mail-order/direct-response advertising where you measure coupon returns. Brand advertising, awareness campaigns, and modern digital engagement metrics were outside his scope.
- **"Times change, people don't"**: Caples himself said this. The psychological principles (self-interest, curiosity, news) are likely timeless. The specific formula wordings (begin with "Introducing," etc.) may feel mechanical if applied literally rather than as structural patterns.
- **No taxonomy of failures**: While the book includes examples of headlines that failed, the formulas themselves are presented as positive patterns. There is less systematic treatment of why bad headlines fail (beyond "they lack self-interest").

### Best Secondary Sources

1. **Lars Lofgren -- "The 35 Headline Formulas of John Caples"** (larslofgren.com/john-caples-headline-formulas/): Complete enumeration of all 35 formulas with groupings and usage guidance. The single best online summary for extraction. Two URLs exist (different slugs, same content).
2. **Bookey.app -- Chapter-by-chapter summary** (bookey.app/book/tested-advertising-methods and /tested-advertising-methods-(5th-edition)): Detailed chapter summaries including Chapter 5 on the 35 formulas. Good for understanding structure without the book.
3. **Digital Heart Marketing -- "106 All-Time Great Headlines"** (digitalheartmarketing.com): Lists 106 tested headlines from Caples with analysis of the three top-level formula categories (self-interest, news, curiosity). Good for examples.
4. **Maverick Words -- "4 Copywriting Lessons"** (maverickwords.com): Clean distillation of Caples's philosophy into four principles: headline primacy, self-interest appeal, substance over style, and relentless testing.
5. **SOFII -- Ken Burnett review** (sofii.org): Honest review acknowledging the book is "dated now" with "clunky and arcane" language, but calling the underlying principles "solid gold." Best source for a balanced take.
6. **Innovaxis -- "Formula for Successful Messaging"** (innovaxisinc.com): Maps Caples's four headline qualities to modern B2B marketing contexts (Google Ads, SEO, email subject lines). Useful for showing continued relevance. Includes examples of headlines that worked AND headlines that failed.

### Relevance Assessment

**Verdict: Strong fit. Caples is THE expert for this topic.**

He is not merely "an" expert on headline formulas -- he is the originator of the systematic, empirically-tested approach to headline categorization. The 35 formulas are directly from his book. The four-quality framework (self-interest > news > curiosity > quick/easy) is his taxonomy. Every modern headline formula list (Copyblogger, HubSpot, etc.) traces back to Caples, whether they cite him or not.

**Specific strengths for the target brief (35 proven headline patterns):**
- He literally created the 35 headline formulas being asked about
- His taxonomy (keyword, benefit, news, price, short) provides the structural categories
- His hierarchy of headline qualities (self-interest first) provides the decision framework for "when to use each"
- His testing methodology (split-test with different return addresses) provides the empirical backing
- His examples of failed headlines provide the "what not to do" counterpoint

**Gaps to fill from other experts:**
- Digital/social media headline adaptation (Caples is pre-internet)
- Emotional trigger taxonomy (Caples covers self-interest broadly but doesn't break it into fear/greed/vanity/etc. as deeply as later copywriters like Schwartz or Cialdini)
- Subject line / push notification / social post headline constraints (character limits, preview text, mobile rendering)
- Modern A/B testing methodology (Caples used mail-order response; modern testing uses click-through rates, heat maps, scroll depth)
