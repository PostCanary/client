## Aaron Draplin -- Deep Research

### Origin Story

Draplin grew up in Detroit, Michigan -- blue-collar Midwest, where "nothing is handed to you." He left at 19 to snowboard in the Pacific Northwest. He could not afford a computer, so he went analog: drawing, painting, learning white-ink cleanup techniques, studying comic books and skateboard/snowboard graphics by hand. In 1996, he washed dishes on a train in Alaska for 16-hour days to save up for his first computer. Once he had vector tools (early Illustrator, FreeHand), he combined his hand-illustration instincts with digital precision.

His aesthetic emerged from two converging forces: (1) practical necessity -- he started in newspaper ad layout, where enormous amounts of information had to be packed into small, fixed physical spaces, and (2) personal obsession with vintage American commercial art he found "junking" -- old signs, dead logos, ration cans, railroad typography, promotional items from the 1940s-1960s. He noticed that mid-century designers like Saul Bass, Paul Rand, Seymour Chwast, Milton Glaser, and Keith Haring all used thick, consistent lines and reductionist forms that "used to line the landscape" but had been abandoned by the design world in favor of complexity.

His "thick lines" style crystallized in 2011 while working on the All Tiny Creatures record for Hometapes. He was experimenting with simple vector shapes, all lined with a consistent thick stroke. He described the result as having "this odd uniformity that was striking, almost Utopian in the feel." The style was not invented -- it was rediscovered from the American vernacular and given modern application. As he puts it: thick lines and bold simplicity are "not a style" but "basic principles of graphic design" that worked in 1966 and work now.

The problem his aesthetic solves: in a design world chasing complexity, gradients, thin hairlines, and decorative effects, most work fails the fundamental test -- can it be reproduced at the size of a pea on a hat AND the size of a building on a wall? His work always passes that test because it never relied on effects that break at different scales or media.

### Core Methodology

1. **Start with research, but don't go too deep.** Have "spirited conversations" with the client. Look at competition and the marketplace online. Then stop. He calls this "informed invention" -- know EXACTLY what the client is trying to accomplish, then put your best foot forward. Over-researching kills originality.

2. **Sketch first, vector second.** All logos start in a Field Notes notebook with a ton of sketches. "Sketch first. Vector that shit up second! Then refine, refine, refine." The pencil produces randomness and vulnerability that pure-digital never achieves. The sketching phase is where invention happens; the vector phase is where precision is applied.

3. **Reduce until only the essential remains.** "There's something beautiful about using the least amount of anything to tell a story." After creating initial solutions, systematically remove elements. If the design still communicates without an element, it did not need that element. He describes this as a funnel: "You dump a pile in the top, and out pops one little refined nugget."

4. **Test at every scale.** "Make it look good at the size of a pea or a softball, and then all the way up to where it works on large, large things." After designing, physically scale it -- shrink it to stamp size, enlarge it to banner size. If it breaks at any size, it is not done. "Ninety-nine times out of 100, now, you're gonna see that logo on your phone, in a little tiny space."

5. **Show it on real things, not white space.** When presenting to clients, never show a logo floating on a white PDF. Show it on a water bottle, on dark space, on a hat, on an envelope. This does two things: demonstrates how the design actually works in the physical world, and loads the client up with applications so they see the value beyond the mark itself.

6. **Use consistent, thick line weights.** Not thin hairlines, not variable-weight trendy strokes. Uniform thickness creates visual cohesion and that "Utopian feel." It also ensures reproducibility -- thick lines survive screen printing, embroidery, small sizes, and low-resolution reproduction where thin lines disappear.

7. **Client ownership supersedes designer ownership.** "In the end, it's their thing. It's my job to make them something they love." Do not force your signature style on a client when it is not appropriate. "I'm not an artist who sells you 'I'm gonna do what I do.' I'm a working designer. If you want Helvetica Light and different colors, I'm designing it for you." The personal style goes into personal projects; client work serves the client's needs.

### Decision Rules (MOST IMPORTANT SECTION)

- If a design element does not survive at stamp size, then remove it. Scalability is the first and non-negotiable test.
- If you can remove an element and the design still communicates, then remove it. Economy of form is the goal.
- If you are presenting to a client, then show the design on physical mockups (hat, bottle, van, envelope), not floating on white space.
- If a design requires gradients, drop shadows, or complex effects to look good, then the underlying form is weak. Redesign the form.
- If the line weights are inconsistent or thin, then thicken and unify them. Thick uniform lines are the signature of something built to last across media.
- If a client comes to you wanting something outside your signature style, then do what they need. Adapt. Reserve your personal aesthetic for personal projects and clients who specifically seek it.
- If you are in round one of presenting, then show fewer options, not more. "You can overwhelm them. I'm learning how to fold that back." Curate down to your strongest solutions and make a case for those.
- If a typeface can be stretched, then never stretch it. "Don't stretch type, ever. Ever!" Use the correct weight or a different face.
- If a design is getting complex, then ask: "Would this work on a sticker? A patch? A hat?" These physical objects are the acid test. If it fails the physical-object test, it is too complex.
- If you are starting a project, then do "informed invention": learn exactly what the client needs, look at the competitive landscape briefly, then stop researching and start making. Over-research kills originality.
- If you are choosing between trend-following and timelessness, then choose timelessness. "Nothing is ever, ever going to challenge a good, solid, legible, readable, scalable, beautiful, crafted mark."
- If you find yourself adding decoration, then stop. "I'm just clearing out the bullshit." Decoration is not design.
- If a design looks like it could only exist on a screen, then it is not done. A design should feel like it belongs on physical objects -- "I want them to feel comfortable in a junk drawer."
- If you are unsure about color, then go bold and saturated, not muted and safe. Draplin's palette is dominated by warm, high-saturation colors (oranges, yellows, warm reds) over cool pastels.
- If a detail seems "out of scope" but would add craft, then do it anyway. "I've gotten jobs simply because of little moments that were 'out of scope' but added up big time in the end."

### Key Concepts and Vocabulary

| Term | What it means | When it applies |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| Thick Lines | Consistent, uniform-weight outlines applied to all vector shapes in a composition. Not a "style" but a fundamental principle rediscovered from mid-century design. | Every logo, icon, illustration, or graphic element. The default treatment. |
| Informed Invention | Research enough to know the client's problem and competitive landscape, then stop and start creating. Avoid over-research that kills originality. | Project kickoff. The research phase should be brief and targeted. |
| Scalability Test | The design must work at the size of a pea and the size of a building. Test by actually scaling it. | Every mark, logo, or icon. Non-negotiable checkpoint before showing work. |
| Economy of Form | Using the absolute minimum number of elements to communicate. "The least amount of anything to tell a story." | Editing phase. After initial creation, strip away until only essentials remain. |
| The Funnel | Creative process metaphor: dump many ideas in the top, refine down to one nugget through iteration and client feedback. | Full project lifecycle from sketching through final delivery. |
| New Nostalgia | Contemporary reinterpretation of vintage styles -- not ironic retro pastiche, but earnest use of proven forms with modern application. Field Notes is the prime example. | Product design, brand identity, any work drawing from Americana heritage. |
| Dead Stuff / Junking | Found objects from thrift stores, flea markets, antique malls -- old signs, logos, promotional items. Primary source of Draplin's visual vocabulary and proof that good design principles are timeless. | Inspiration and reference gathering. The American vernacular as design library. |
| Bold Minimalism | "Turning restraint into refinement." Clean, simple lines and shapes, minimal text, focus on functionality. Minimal decoration, emphasis on negative space and tight color palette. | Layout and composition decisions. The overarching aesthetic philosophy. |
| Client Ownership | The final piece belongs to the client, not the designer. The designer's job is to make the client love it, not to impose ego. | Client relationships, presentation, and revision process. |

### Specific Techniques

**Logo design process (step by step):**
1. Have a focused conversation with the client. Understand exactly what they need to accomplish.
2. Brief online research: competition, marketplace, existing visual landscape. Do not go deep.
3. Open a Field Notes notebook. Sketch heavily -- lots of variations, exploring shapes, letterforms, arrangements. This is fast, loose, generative.
4. Open Adobe Illustrator. Vector up the strongest sketches. Apply consistent thick line weights to all elements.
5. Refine, refine, refine. Simplify shapes. Remove anything unnecessary.
6. Scale test: shrink to pea size, enlarge to billboard size. If anything breaks, fix it or remove it.
7. Build a PDF presentation showing multiple pages of solutions -- but curate. Do not overwhelm with 50 options.
8. Show each solution applied to real contexts: on a water bottle, on a hat, on dark background, on light background, at small size, at large size.
9. Incorporate client feedback. Remember: "client ownership supersedes designer ownership."
10. Final delivery: keep the file tight. "Take the extra time to keep that file tight. Whatever element I was a little lazy with came back to bite me in the end."

**Making something look "real" (physical/print) vs. digital/SaaS:**
1. Use thick, consistent outlines -- not thin hairlines that read as "digital."
2. Use solid color fields and shapes -- not gradients, shadows, or transparency effects.
3. Choose workhorse typefaces (Futura Bold, Spartan) used un-ironically -- the same faces that appear on railroad signs and old signage. Not trendy display faces.
4. Design as if it will be screen-printed, embroidered, or stamped. These physical reproduction methods demand simplicity, limited colors, and strong forms.
5. Test by imagining the design on a patch, a sticker, a hat, a notebook, an envelope. If it could not exist as any of these, it does not have the physical-object DNA.
6. Reference the American vernacular: the visual language of old hardware stores, state parks, seed packets, and mid-century commercial art. These are the visual cues that signal "real business" rather than "digital startup."
7. Add small craft moments -- details that are "out of scope" but show care. In Field Notes: stitched bindings, specific paper stocks, print finishes you can feel.

**Color approach:**
1. Default to bold, saturated colors. Draplin's palette centers on oranges, warm yellows, deep reds, with contrasting darks (navy, black).
2. Use color blocks, not gradients. Flat fills, not soft transitions.
3. Limit the palette. Fewer colors = more impact = better reproducibility on physical media.
