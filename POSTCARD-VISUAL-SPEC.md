# PostCanary Postcard Visual Specification — Prompt-Ready Design Contract

> **Purpose:** This document describes, in extreme detail, what professional HVAC direct mail postcards look like — every element, font, color, proportion, and spacing rule — so that an AI building Vue postcard templates can produce output indistinguishable from a professional print shop's work. Written from analysis of PostcardMania, Mail Shark, and Wise Pelican HVAC postcards + expert guidance from Joy Gendusa (PostcardMania CEO/founder, former graphic designer, 27 years, 125K+ clients, 2B+ postcards mailed).

---

## Expert Source Summary

### Joy Gendusa — PostcardMania (primary authority)
- **Headline:** Must fill 15-25% of the front. One central unmistakable message. Big. Bold. Not cluttered with other text. The headline IS the front — everything else supports it.
- **12 Elements Checklist:** (1) Clear bold headline, (2) Headline-supporting graphic, (3) Color that POPS, (4) Subheadline on back leading into text, (5) Scannable bullet points, (6) No jargon, (7) Benefits not features, (8) A specific offer, (9) CTA in a BRIGHT NON-MATCHING color, (10) Return address, (11) Company name + logo, (12) Map for local business
- **Font guidance:** Each font has a personality. Sans-serif for modern/confident. The effect of the font must match the purpose. High-contrast color combinations make words JUMP off the card. CTA like "Call Now!" should use a bright color (orange bg + white text) that appears NOWHERE else on the card.
- **Eye trail:** The design should lead the recipient effortlessly through the text. People in photos should face TOWARD the text (not away from it). Placement of copy and images creates a reading path.
- **"Design is not color choice or font selection — that is STYLE. Design is the arrangement of elements."** The RIGHT elements must all be present and work with each other.

### CRST Direct Mail
- **3-second readability test:** If a stranger can't identify your offer in 3 seconds, redesign.
- **Brand logo — corner only.** Not dominating, not center.
- **One hero image, full bleed.** Not two images competing.
- **Single dominant headline with offer** on the front.
- **Phone number in LARGE type** on the back.
- **Visual hierarchy:** Headline → Image → Supporting copy → CTA. In that order.
- **Avoid:** Gradient backgrounds behind small text, all-caps paragraphs, multiple font sizes competing for dominance.

### Mailpro.org (EDDM design guide)
- **Headline:** At least 24pt font, preferably 36pt+ for maximum visibility. 5-8 words max. Bold sans-serif reads faster than serif or script. High contrast against background.
- **Phone numbers should be large and memorable.**
- **QR code minimum 1"×1"** for reliable scanning.
- **Paper stock:** 14pt-16pt coated cardstock.
- **300 DPI minimum** for all images.

## FRONT SIDE — Detailed Element Breakdown

### Element 1: HERO PHOTO
**What the pros do:**
- The photo is ALWAYS industry-specific. HVAC cards show: AC units on the side of a house, a technician in a branded polo shirt with a tool bag, a family sitting comfortably on a couch with the AC vent visible, a house exterior in summer heat. NEVER: abstract images, stock photos of people at computers, generic cityscapes, or anything unrelated to heating/cooling/home comfort.
- The photo occupies 40-65% of the front card area on most PostcardMania templates. It is NOT always full-bleed edge-to-edge — many of the HVAC templates use a SPLIT layout where the photo occupies one defined zone and a solid color block occupies another. Full-bleed is ONE option, not the only option.
- Photo quality: bright, well-lit, high-contrast. Daylight scenes dominate. Dark or moody photos are rare — the industry targets homeowners during daytime mailbox check.
- When people are in the photo: they are SMILING, comfortable, in a HOME setting. When technicians are in the photo: they are UNIFORMED, professional, holding tools or standing next to equipment. They are NOT candid/artistic — they are posed for confidence.

**What ours has:**
- A stock photo of a person sitting at a desk looking at COMPUTER CODE on a monitor. This communicates "software developer" not "HVAC company." It is the single most damaging element on our card. A homeowner would not understand what this business does by looking at this photo.

**The fix:**
- Replace with an industry-appropriate image: an HVAC technician in work clothes, an AC unit on a house exterior, a happy family at home, or equipment being serviced. If no real Desert Diamond photos are available, use an Unsplash/Pexels photo tagged "HVAC technician" or "air conditioning repair" or "home comfort family."

---

### Element 2: HEADLINE
**What the pros do:**
- **Size:** The headline is the LARGEST text element on the front. In PostcardMania examples, headlines fill 15-25% of the front area. On a 9×6" card, that means the headline text block is roughly 2.25" × 4.5" (or equivalent proportional area). Actual rendered font sizes appear to be 36-48pt for the primary headline words.
- **Weight:** 800-900 (ExtraBold or Black). Never Regular, Medium, or even Bold (700). The heaviest available weight in the font family.
- **Font family:** Condensed sans-serif is dominant across PostcardMania HVAC cards. Think: Impact, Bebas Neue, Oswald, Barlow Condensed, Anton, or similar tight condensed grotesques. These fonts pack more words into less horizontal space while remaining LOUD. Body text uses a contrasting geometric sans (Montserrat, Open Sans, Lato, Inter).
- **Color:** Headlines are almost always WHITE on a dark/saturated background (navy, dark blue, brand-dark) OR a DARK color on a light/white background. HIGH CONTRAST is mandatory — the headline must be readable from 3+ feet away.
- **Content:** 4-8 words max. Specific. Benefit-focused. Location-included when possible. Examples from the PostcardMania HVAC gallery:
  - "THE SUMMER HEAT IS HERE" (5 words)
  - "NEW A/C SYSTEM!" (3 words)
  - "HEATING & COOLING" (2 words)
  - "Your local heating & cooling experts!" (6 words)
  - "KEEP YOUR FAMILY SAFE & COMFORTABLE" (6 words)
  - "IMPORTANT REMINDER" (2 words)
- **Position:** Top 40% of the card, or spanning a solid color zone that occupies one side/section of the card. NEVER buried at the bottom under a photo. The headline is the FIRST thing you see.
- **Treatment:** Often ALL-CAPS. Sometimes Title Case for friendlier tones. NEVER lowercase.
- **Multiple sizes within the headline:** Several PostcardMania cards use 2-3 different text sizes WITHIN the headline block. Example: "THE SUMMER HEAT IS HERE" at 28pt, then "NEW A/C SYSTEM!" below it at 42pt in a different color. The most important phrase is BIGGEST.

**What ours has:**
- "Phoenix Homeowners: Your AC Tune-" — TRUNCATED. The headline gets cut off mid-word because the bottom bar is too short for the text at the current font size. This looks broken, not designed.
- The headline sits in the bottom 36% dark bar over the photo. In the PostcardMania references, headlines that sit over photos are either: (a) in the TOP portion of the card with the photo behind them, or (b) inside a SOLID COLOR ZONE that gives them a clean reading surface — not cramped at the bottom competing with a phone number band.

**The fix:**
- Shorten the headline to 4-6 words max: "Your AC Tune-Up Is Due" (6 words — drop "Phoenix Homeowners:") or keep the location but shorten: "Phoenix: AC Tune-Up Time" (4 words).
- OR increase the bottom bar height to 45-50% so the headline has room.
- OR move the headline to the TOP of the card over the photo with a semi-transparent dark overlay, and use the bottom for phone only.
- Use a CONDENSED sans-serif font (Oswald, Bebas Neue, Barlow Condensed) so more words fit in less space.

### Element 3: COMPANY LOGO / IDENTITY
**What the pros do:**
- The logo is a REAL GRAPHIC MARK — not plain text in a box. It has visual complexity: an icon, custom lettering, brand symbol, or combination mark. PostcardMania's "ABC" example logos show a designed wordmark with custom typography and color, not system-font text in a rectangle.
- **Size:** 0.75-1.5" wide. Small enough not to dominate (Gendusa: "shouldn't overshadow the offer") but large enough to be immediately recognizable.
- **Placement:** Usually top-left or bottom-left corner. Sometimes inset in its own WHITE CONTAINER (a white rectangle or circle with a thin border) to give it visual mass against a photo or colored background.
- **Treatment:** The logo sits in a CLEAR ZONE — it has breathing room around it. It's not crammed against text or photo edges. Often has a white or semi-transparent background pad behind it so it reads against any background.

**What ours has:**
- "DESERT DIAMOND HVAC" in plain sans-serif text inside a small solid blue rectangle. This looks like a UI label or a tag, not a business identity. It has no visual personality. A real HVAC company logo would have: a diamond shape (matching the name), maybe an AC unit icon, custom lettering, multiple colors.

**The fix:**
- Since we don't have Desert Diamond's real logo (Firecrawl couldn't scrape it), the wordmark fallback needs to be MORE VISUALLY SUBSTANTIAL:
  - Increase size to minimum 1.0" wide
  - Use a heavier, more distinctive font (Impact, Anton, or a condensed black weight)
  - Add visual interest: a DIAMOND SHAPE icon before the text, or set the text on TWO LINES ("DESERT DIAMOND" on line 1, "HVAC" on line 2 in a different size/weight)
  - Use a background pad of at least 0.12in on all sides
  - Consider white text on the brand color (current approach) but with a THICKER container that reads as a branded element, not a tag

---

### Element 4: OFFER BADGE / PRICE CALLOUT
**What the pros do:**
- Price callouts are LARGE and UNMISSABLE. PostcardMania uses: circular BURSTS (1.0-1.5" diameter) in bright RED or ORANGE with white text at 24-30pt. The price inside the burst is the dominant text — "$79", "$50 OFF", "FREE."
- HAC-1004 has THREE price circles side by side: "$79 $99 $199" — each its own colored circle, creating a menu-style pricing strip.
- Colors: ALWAYS a high-contrast, attention-grabbing color. RED, ORANGE, YELLOW on dark. NEVER the same color as the main brand palette — it should POP differently from everything else.
- Position: Top-right corner (ribbon style), mid-right (burst style), or bottom of the front (price strip). It should be readable without conflicting with the headline.

**What ours has:**
- "$79 TUNE-UP" ribbon badge in the top-right. This is structurally correct (ribbon placement, angled, Draplin shadow). Could be more impactful — the text might be too small relative to the PostcardMania bursts.

**The fix:**
- Consider making the badge LARGER (1.25-1.5" diameter if burst, wider ribbon if ribbon style). The price should be readable from arm's length.

---

### Element 5: PHONE NUMBER (FRONT)
**What the pros do:**
- Phone on the front sits inside a SOLID FILLED BAR or CONTAINER spanning significant width. Color is HIGH-CONTRAST — often a bright accent color (RED, ORANGE) that is DIFFERENT from the headline color.
- **Size:** 22-28pt on the front. Large enough to be noticed but NOT competing with the headline for dominance.
- The phone is in the BOTTOM 15-20% of the card. It's the last thing the eye hits in the Z-pattern (after logo, headline, offer badge).
- Joy Gendusa: "CTA in a bright NON-MATCHING color." The phone/CTA container should be a color that appears NOWHERE else on the card — this makes it a visual anomaly that draws the eye.

**What ours has:**
- Full-width blue phone band at the very bottom. The color (#0488F5 — Desert Diamond blue) is the SAME as the wordmark — so the phone and the logo are the same color. This violates Gendusa's "non-matching color" rule for CTAs. The phone and the wordmark visually merge instead of each being distinct.

**The fix:**
- Change the phone bar to a DIFFERENT color from the wordmark. Options: Desert Diamond orange (#F97B22) for the phone bar (warm/urgent), keep blue for the wordmark (cool/trust). OR use RED/a deeper orange for the phone bar. The phone bar should be the ONE element in a unique color.

---

### Element 6: COLOR ZONES
**What the pros do:**
- Pro postcards are divided into 2-3 MAJOR COLOR ZONES. Not a photo with a thin stripe — but genuinely LARGE blocks of color occupying entire sections of the card.
- Common PostcardMania front layouts:
  - **50/50 split:** Left half = solid deep color (navy, dark blue) with headline text. Right half = photo. Sharp vertical division.
  - **60/40 horizontal:** Top 60% = photo. Bottom 40% = solid color bar with headline + phone. Clean horizontal division.
  - **Full coverage:** Entire card is a solid BRIGHT color (yellow, orange) with text — no photo at all (the "official notice" style like HAC-1026). This works for urgency/reminder cards.
  - **Photo + inset:** Full-bleed photo with text elements INSET in colored containers (colored rectangles placed ON the photo, each carrying text). The containers make the text readable and add color mass.
- The COLOR IS SATURATED. Not tinted, not pastel, not muted. Deep navy (#0A1F44 range), bright red (#CC0000 range), warm orange (#F57C00 range), bright yellow (#FFD600 range). Direct mail colors are LOUDER than web colors.

**What ours has:**
- Photo in the top 64%, dark bar in the bottom 36%, blue phone band below the dark bar. This is a 3-layer sandwich (photo / dark / blue) where the dark layer and the blue layer are too similar in value (both are dark-ish), so they blend into one undifferentiated bottom section. The photo occupies too much of the card relative to the content zones.

**The fix:**
- Either: (a) Increase the solid color zone to 45-50% of the card so it has visual MASS, or (b) split the card into a true 50/50 (photo left, colored content right) or 60/40 (photo top, big color bar bottom), or (c) use CONTRASTING colors for the headline zone vs phone zone (dark navy headline bar + bright orange phone bar = two distinct readable zones).

## BACK SIDE — Detailed Element Breakdown

### Element 7: JOHNSON BOX (OFFER BLOCK)
**What the pros do:**
- Solid brand-color headline bar spanning the full width of the offer box. The bar is visually HEAVY — 0.3-0.5" tall. Contains the offer headline in WHITE BOLD text at 22-28pt.
- White interior with a visible BORDER (2pt solid brand color).
- Value stack items use checkmarks (✓) in brand color, left-aligned label, right-aligned dollar value. 3-5 items.
- Customer price/savings at the bottom in a LARGER size than the items (20-24pt). This is the conversion trigger.
- Deadline in BOLD, URGENT color (red/orange) inside the box — not a footer footnote.

**What ours has:**
- This is actually CORRECT structurally. Blue headline bar, white interior, checkmark items, deadline as orange urgency bar. The Johnson Box is the strongest element on our card.

---

### Element 8: PHONE / CTA BLOCK (BACK)
**What the pros do:**
- Phone number is the SINGLE LARGEST TEXT ELEMENT on the entire back side. 32-40pt. It's bigger than the offer headline.
- "CALL NOW" or "CALL TODAY" label is PART OF the phone block — same container, no visual gap. The label + phone read as one unit.
- The entire CTA block is a SOLID FILLED COLOR — the brand's primary or accent color, spanning the full content column width.
- Website URL below in smaller text (12-14pt).
- QR code adjacent, 0.75-1.0" square, with white background pad.

**What ours has:**
- "(623) 246-2377" at 32pt — correct size. "CALL NOW" underlined and integrated — correct. Orange CTA block — correct. QR code — correct. This section is structurally sound.

---

### Element 9: TRUST BADGES
**What the pros do:**
- Trust badges are either: (a) REAL BRAND LOGOS — the actual BBB shield, the actual Angi logo mark, the actual HomeAdvisor checkmark — rendered as images, not text; or (b) STYLED TEXT BADGES in the correct brand colors (filled, not outlined) when image logos aren't available.
- The badges form a HORIZONTAL ROW at the bottom of the content column.
- Each badge is legible — either a recognizable icon or SHORT text (max 8-10 characters per badge: "BBB A+", "ANGI", "NATE", "LIC & INS").
- "Licensed & Insured" is ALWAYS present for home services. It carries the same visual weight as the brand badges.

**What ours has:**
- Filled color blocks (correct color approach) but the labels are TRUNCATED: "ANGI CERTIF...", "HOMEADVIS...", "LICENSED & I..." — ellipsis text makes them unreadable.

**The fix:**
- Use SHORT labels that fit: "BBB A+" (6 chars), "ANGI" (4 chars), "HOME ADV" (8 chars) or just "HA" with the full icon, "LIC & INS" (9 chars). Or increase the badge size so full text fits.
- Better yet: use the SVG artwork (even our placeholder SVGs) as `<img>` elements so the graphic carries the brand identity — the text label becomes secondary or hidden when the image is present.

---

### Element 10: RATING / REVIEW
**What the pros do:**
- Star rating rendered as actual star icons (filled stars in gold/amber). Not a number alone.
- Review count displayed prominently: "2,423 customers" or "127 five-star reviews."
- A SPECIFIC customer quote in italics, attributed by name: *"Fixed our AC in under an hour on a 108-degree day." — Sarah M.*
- This block occupies its own visual zone — not crammed between other elements.

**What ours has:**
- ★★★★★ 4.9/5 — 2423 customers. Review quote present but truncated with "..." This is structurally correct but the review text truncation weakens it.

## TYPOGRAPHY SPECIFICATION (for CSS implementation)

### Headline Font
- **Family:** A CONDENSED sans-serif. Recommended: `'Oswald', 'Barlow Condensed', 'Bebas Neue', Impact, sans-serif`
- **Weight:** 800-900 (ExtraBold or Black)
- **Size (front):** 36-48pt for the primary headline phrase. If using 2-tier headline (small lead-in + large main phrase), lead-in at 18-22pt and main phrase at 36-48pt.
- **Size (back offer headline):** 22-28pt
- **Letter-spacing:** 0 to -0.02em (TIGHT, not loose)
- **Line-height:** 0.95-1.05 (tight)
- **Transform:** uppercase
- **Color:** White on dark backgrounds. Brand-dark on white backgrounds. NEVER medium-tone on medium-tone.

### Body / Item Font
- **Family:** A clean geometric sans-serif. Recommended: `'Inter', 'Montserrat', 'Open Sans', 'Lato', sans-serif`
- **Weight:** 400-600 for body, 700 for emphasis
- **Size:** 11-13pt for value stack items, 10-11pt for fine print / return address
- **Color:** #333333 on white backgrounds, white on colored backgrounds

### Phone Number Font
- **Family:** Same as headline (condensed sans) or a heavier variant
- **Weight:** 800-900
- **Size (front):** 22-28pt
- **Size (back):** 32-40pt — BIGGEST text on the back
- **Letter-spacing:** 0.02-0.04em (slightly tracked for readability at large sizes)
- **Color:** White on brand-colored background

### Badge / Label Font
- **Family:** Same as body font
- **Weight:** 700-800
- **Size:** 10-12pt
- **Transform:** uppercase
- **Letter-spacing:** 0.04-0.08em (tracked for small caps feel)

## COLOR SPECIFICATION

### For HVAC / Home Services specifically:
- **Primary trust color:** Deep navy or blue (#0A1F44 to #1A3A6A range). Communicates trust, professionalism, reliability.
- **Urgency/CTA accent:** Bright orange (#F57C00), warm red (#CC3300), or bright yellow (#FFD600). This is the "non-matching" CTA color per Gendusa. Used ONLY for: phone bar, CTA button, offer badge, deadline text.
- **Neutral background:** White (#FFFFFF) for content areas. No grey tints, no off-whites — pure white for maximum text contrast.
- **Text on dark:** #FFFFFF (pure white). Never #F0F0F0 or #E5E5E5 — opacity-reduced text is a web pattern, not a print pattern.
- **Text on light:** #1A1A1A or #333333. Never #666666 or lighter — that's unreadable at arm's length.

### Desert Diamond specific:
- **Brand blue:** #0488F5 (their extracted primary). Use this for: Johnson Box headline bar, wordmark background, trust/stability elements.
- **Brand orange:** #F97B22 (their extracted accent). Use this for: phone CTA bar, offer badge, deadline urgency bar. This is the "non-matching" Gendusa color.
- **DON'T use the same color for the wordmark AND the phone bar.** They should be visually distinct — the wordmark is blue (trust), the phone bar is orange (action).

### B. Font Loading — How to Import a Condensed Headline Font

The project currently uses `Instrument Sans` loaded via Google Fonts:
```css
@import url("https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Instrument+Sans:wght@400;500;600;700&display=swap");
```

**To add a condensed headline font (Oswald recommended):**

Add to `src/styles/index.css` after the existing import:
```css
@import url("https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Oswald:wght@500;600;700&display=swap");
```

Then in `src/styles/print-scale.css`, add a new token:
```css
--pc-headline-family: 'Oswald', 'Impact', 'Arial Narrow', sans-serif;
```

And update `.pc-headline` to use it:
```css
.pc-headline {
  font-family: var(--pc-headline-family);
  /* existing size/weight rules stay */
}
```

**Why Oswald:**
- Free on Google Fonts, widely supported
- Condensed proportions — packs 30-40% more characters per line than regular sans-serif
- Available in weights 200-700 (we need 600-700 for headlines)
- Used by real print shops and PostcardMania-style designs
- Clean, modern, LOUD at large sizes — not decorative, not cute

**Alternatives:** Barlow Condensed (slightly softer), Bebas Neue (all-caps only, even more condensed), Anton (very bold but limited weights).

### E. Two-Tier Headline Pattern (from PostcardMania references)

Many PostcardMania HVAC cards use a TWO-TIER headline, not a single line:

```
Line 1 (small, 16-20pt, normal weight, white):
"Phoenix Homeowners:"

Line 2 (large, 36-48pt, 800 weight, white or accent color):
"YOUR AC TUNE-UP IS DUE"
```

This solves the truncation problem because:
- Line 1 is smaller and fits easily
- Line 2 is the SHORT punch (5-6 words max in condensed font)
- Combined they have more visual IMPACT than a single medium-sized line

Implementation in Vue:
```vue
<div class="pc-headline-block">
  <div class="pc-headline-lead" :style="{ fontSize: '16pt', fontWeight: 500, opacity: 0.9 }">
    Phoenix Homeowners:
  </div>
  <div class="pc-headline" :style="{ fontSize: '40pt', fontWeight: 700, fontFamily: 'var(--pc-headline-family)' }">
    YOUR AC TUNE-UP IS DUE
  </div>
</div>
```

### F. What "Eye Trail" Actually Means in CSS (Gendusa's #16 element)

The Z-pattern isn't just about POSITION — it's about VISUAL WEIGHT creating a reading path:

```
1. LOGO (top-left) — medium weight, brand recognition
   ↓ eye naturally scans right
2. OFFER BADGE (top-right) — HIGH weight, bright color, pulls eye
   ↓ eye drops diagonally
3. HEADLINE (bottom-left area of solid bar) — HIGHEST weight, largest text
   ↓ eye scans right
4. PHONE (bottom-right or full-width bottom) — HIGH weight, contrasting color
```

Each element must be HEAVIER (bigger, bolder, brighter) than the previous one in the reading path to pull the eye forward. If the phone bar is the same color as the wordmark, the eye doesn't get pulled — it stays at the wordmark.

### G. Print vs Screen Color Note

Colors on screen (RGB at 96dpi) appear BRIGHTER and more saturated than the same colors printed (CMYK at 300dpi on matte stock). What looks "too loud" on screen will look "just right" in print. What looks "just right" on screen will look DULL in print.

**Rule:** Design 10-15% more saturated than you think looks good on screen. PostcardMania's templates look almost garish on their website — but they look PERFECT on the matte cardstock that arrives in your mailbox. This is intentional.

Our current renders might look "too orange" or "too blue" on Drake's monitor — that's correct for print. Do NOT mute the colors to make them look elegant on screen.
